## Core Philosophy

### Evidence-Based, Not Dogmatic
Use Garner's **Superstition Test** to distinguish real rules from false ones: (1) Does following this rule improve clarity? (2) Do educated writers follow it? (3) Does breaking it confuse readers? If "no" to all three, it's a superstition. Common superstitions include: never split infinitives, never end with prepositions, never begin with "And/But." (Sources: Garner, Le Guin)

### Reader-Centered
"Writing is good if readers find it easy to follow; writing is bad if readers find it hard to follow." (Garner) "Pity the readers"—make their job easy. (Vonnegut) Reader expectations drive comprehension. (Gopen & Swan)

### Balanced Approach
"Sound grammatical but relaxed, refined but natural, correct but unpedantic." (Garner) "One foot inside the box, one foot outside"—balance convention and innovation. (Hartenberger) Rules are diagnostic guides, not absolute laws. (Le Guin)

## Key Principles by Category

### Sentences & Syntax

**Sentence Length & Rhythm**
- "There is no optimum sentence length. The optimum is variety." (Le Guin) Mix short (3-8 words), medium (10-20), and occasional long (25+) sentences.
- Hemingway emphasizes short, punchy sentences for clarity; Provost warns against monotony from similar lengths; Gopen & Swan suggest length should match stress-position capacity.
- **Practical resolution**: Default to brevity, but vary for rhythm. If important information competes for emphasis, split sentences or use colons/semicolons. (Full discussion: summarized.md Section III)

**Parallel Structure**
Use parallel grammatical structures for rhythm and emphasis when listing items, actions, or ideas. (The Economist)

**Repetition**
"Repetition is not merely permissible; it is essential to narrative power." Use deliberate repetition for rhythm and emphasis—don't avoid it out of false variety. (Le Guin)

### Word Choice & Diction

**Precision Over Generality**
Replace generic words with precise ones: "said angrily" → "snapped"; "went quickly" → "raced"; "a dog" → "a border collie." (The Economist, Strunk & White)

**Anglo-Saxon Over Latinate**
"Utilize" → "use"; "commence" → "start"; "terminate" → "end." "If the same idea can be expressed in a simple way or in a complex way, the simple way is better—and, paradoxically, it will typically lead readers to conclude that the writer is smarter." (Garner, Orwell, Vonnegut)

**Cut Clutter**
"Clutter is the disease of American writing." (Zinsser) Cut: redundant modifiers, unnecessary qualifiers ("very," "quite," "basically"), pompous language, "the fact that," "in order to" → "to." Aim to cut 25-50% of first drafts. "Brevity enhances speed, clarity, and impact." (Garner, Strunk & White)

**Preserve Useful Distinctions**
Maintain distinctions between easily confused words: imply/infer, affect/effect, flaunt/flout, disinterested/uninterested. (Garner)

**Avoid Skunked Terms**
Words like "comprise" have become semantically unstable—different readers interpret them differently. Use unambiguous alternatives. (Garner)

**Eliminate Verbal False Limbs**
"Make contact with" → "contact"; "exhibit a tendency to" → "tends to"; "give rise to" → "causes." (Orwell)

### Sentence Structure & Reader Expectations

**Stress Position (Sentence End)**
Place most important new information at sentence end—readers automatically emphasize it. Avoid weak endings like "however," "respectively," "as mentioned above." (Gopen & Swan, Strunk & White)

**Topic Position (Sentence Beginning)**
Start sentences with familiar information connecting to what came before. Pattern: old → new. (Gopen & Swan)

**Keep Subject and Verb Together**
Separating subject from verb by more than 5-7 words forces readers to hold grammatical structure in memory. (Gopen & Swan, Strunk & White)

**Actions in Verbs, Not Nouns**
Convert nominalizations to verbs: "We performed an analysis" → "We analyzed"; "The inhibition of X by Y" → "Y inhibits X." (Gopen & Swan, Owens)

**Passive Voice: Use Deliberately**
"Where it does belong, we ought to use it freely. It is one of the lovely versatilities of the verb." Use passive when: (1) receiver more important than doer; (2) doer unknown/irrelevant; (3) emphasizing action itself. Don't avoid dogmatically. (Le Guin)

### Academic Writing & Problem Construction

**Construct a Problem, Not Just a Thesis**
Before presenting your argument, demonstrate that readers' current understanding has a flaw, gap, or inadequacy. (McEnerney)

**Tripartite Problem Grammar**
Every problem needs: (1) **Instability**—something amiss or contradictory; (2) **Costs/Benefits to readers**—not to you; (3) **Discourse community**—who cares? (McEnerney)

**Avoid the Gap Trap**
"No one has studied X" often fails because readers have no reason to care. Reframe as error: "Current understanding assumes Z, but this is flawed." (McEnerney)

**Five-Part Introduction Structure**
(1) Task; (2) State-of-the-Art; (3) Flaw; (4) Your Solution; (5) Proof. (Cham)

**One Idea Is Better Than Many**
Introductions should coalesce around one dominant contribution. (Cham, McEnerney)

### Show, Don't Tell (With Nuance)

**Core Principle**
Replace abstract emotions with concrete imagery: "She was nervous" → "She twisted the ring on her finger." (Hemingway, Provost, Strunk & White)

**Important Caveat**
"Show don't tell" is a diagnostic guide for beginners, not an absolute law. "Expanded into laws, they are, as Rechy says, nonsense." Exposition, description, and narrator voice are legitimate tools. Hardy opens *Return of the Native* with pure landscape description. Use this rule diagnostically when prose feels vague, but don't apply dogmatically. (Le Guin)

### Voice & Writer Presence

**Sound Like Yourself**
"The writing style which is most natural for you is bound to echo the speech you heard when a child." (Vonnegut) Voice emerges from accumulated consistent choices across register, sentence patterns, diction, scene/summary balance. (Hartenberger)

**Find Subjects You Care About**
"It is this genuine caring, and not your games with language, which will be the most compelling and seductive element in your style." (Vonnegut) Don't write what you think others want. (Zinsser)

**Place Yourself in Background**
Draw attention to sense and substance, not to yourself. Cut "I think," "in my opinion," "it seems to me." (Strunk & White)

**Intentionality**
"What distinguishes good writing from competent writing is intentionality—making purposeful, context-aware decisions about when to follow conventions and when to break them." (Hartenberger)

### Openings & Endings

**Strong Leads**
"The most important sentence in any article is the first one." (Zinsser) Use either: direct reader relevance OR a specific human in a concrete situation. (Provost)

**Know When to Stop**
"An article that doesn't stop where it should stop becomes a drag and therefore a failure." Try deleting the last paragraph—does it work better? (Zinsser)

### Revision Process

**Good Writing = Rewritten Writing**
"Rewriting is the essence of writing well: it's where the game is won or lost." (Zinsser) Expect 10+ passes. (Raibert) "THE CREATIVE PROCESS: INSPIRATION, CREATION, SELF-LOATHING." (Cham)

**Start with Permission to Write Badly**
First drafts prioritize getting ideas down. Fragment sentences and messy organization are acceptable initially. (Raibert)

**Read Aloud**
"The test of a sentence is, Does it sound right?" Trust your ear over rules. (Le Guin, Raibert)

**Kill Your Darlings**
"If a sentence, no matter how excellent, does not illuminate your subject in some new and useful way, scratch it out." (Vonnegut)

## Essential Diagnostic Tests

Apply these tests systematically. For the complete set of 80+ tests with full methodology, see summarized.md Section II.

### Structural Tests

| Test | Question | Method | Source |
|------|----------|--------|--------|
| **Stress Position** | Does important info land at sentence end? | Check last 3-5 words of each sentence | Gopen & Swan |
| **Topic Flow** | Does each sentence begin with familiar info? | List topic positions; check for connection | Gopen & Swan |
| **Subject-Verb Separation** | How many words between subject and verb? | Count; revise if >7 words | Gopen & Swan |
| **Paragraph Unity** | Does every sentence serve one main idea? | State paragraph's point in one sentence | Raibert, Economist |
| **Old/New Flow** | old → new → that becomes old → newer? | Mark [O] and [N] for each sentence | Gopen & Swan |

### Clarity Tests

| Test | Question | Method | Source |
|------|----------|--------|--------|
| **Clutter Audit** | Can I cut 20-30% of words? | Try removing words; keep meaning | Strunk & White, Zinsser |
| **Active/Passive Ratio** | <20% passive voice? | Count passives; check justification | Owens, Strunk & White |
| **Nominalization** | Actions in verbs or hidden in nouns? | List verbs; look for -tion, -ment nouns | Gopen & Swan |
| **Reader Sympathy** | Made this as easy as possible? | Read as tired, busy reader | Vonnegut |
| **Precision** | Each word the most accurate choice? | Check key sentences for ambiguity | Vonnegut |

### Word Choice Tests

| Test | Question | Method | Source |
|------|----------|--------|--------|
| **Pretentious Diction** | Latinate where Anglo-Saxon works? | Find -tion, -ment, -ize words; simplify | Orwell |
| **Generic Word Audit** | Using precise or placeholder words? | Circle "said," "went," "got"; replace | Economist |
| **Dying Metaphor** | Can I visualize this metaphor? | If you can't picture it, cut it | Orwell |
| **Skunked Term** | Is meaning contested? | Check if word confuses readers | Garner |
| **Adverb Audit** | Can I delete -ly words? | Search "ly "; delete or strengthen verb | Hemingway |

### Show/Tell Tests

| Test | Question | Method | Source |
|------|----------|--------|--------|
| **Observable Facts** | Could this be filmed with a camera? | If no, it's telling not showing | Hemingway |
| **Subtext Check** | Remove emotion words—does meaning remain? | Delete explicit emotions; check | Hemingway |
| **Concrete/Abstract Ratio** | 60%+ concrete nouns? | Count and categorize nouns | Hemingway, Strunk & White |
| **Specificity** | Specific details or vague generalities? | Replace "a dog" → "a border collie" | Provost |

### Voice & Style Tests

| Test | Question | Method | Source |
|------|----------|--------|--------|
| **Mind's Ear** | Does it sound right aloud? | Read aloud; trust your ear | Le Guin |
| **Authentic Voice** | Sounds like me or imitation? | Compare to natural speech | Vonnegut |
| **Sentence Variety** | Length varies or monotonous? | Count words in 10 sentences | Provost, Le Guin |
| **Genuine Care** | Do I care about this subject? | If mechanical, find angle you care about | Vonnegut, Zinsser |
| **Intentionality** | Made this choice deliberately? | Ask "why this over alternatives?" | Hartenberger |

### Rule Application Tests

| Test | Question | Method | Source |
|------|----------|--------|--------|
| **Superstition Test** | Does rule serve clarity or is it tradition? | Check: improves clarity? Educated writers follow? Breaking confuses? | Garner |
| **Rule-as-Guide** | Diagnostic tool or dogmatic law? | Use rules to identify problems, not as absolute constraints | Le Guin |
| **Grammatical but Relaxed** | Correct but not pedantic? | Read aloud—natural or performing "proper English"? | Garner |

### Academic/Technical Tests

| Test | Question | Method | Source |
|------|----------|--------|--------|
| **Cost/Benefit** | Benefits reader or just writer? | Check if problem matters to readers | McEnerney |
| **Three-Component** | Instability + Costs + Community? | Mark [I], [C/B], [COM] in intro | McEnerney |
| **Bean-Spilling** | Stated conclusions or just hinted? | Highlight actual findings in intro | Raibert |
| **Five-Part Structure** | Task → State-of-Art → Flaw → Solution → Proof? | Label each paragraph's function | Cham |

## Feedback Structure

### 1. Read the Writing Completely
Read the provided file before analyzing.

### 2. Apply Diagnostic Tests
Use tests above systematically. Identify patterns across multiple diagnostics.

### 3. Provide Feedback in This Format

#### Overall Assessment
2-3 sentence summary of strengths and primary improvement areas.

#### Major Issues (Priority Order)

For each major issue:
- **Issue**: Clear statement of problem
- **Why it matters**: Effect on readers
- **Diagnostic test(s)**: Which test revealed this
- **Source guidance**: Cite relevant principle with source
- **Specific examples**: 2-3 concrete instances from text
- **Recommendation**: Actionable fix with before/after if possible

#### Secondary Issues
Brief bullets: Issue + quick fix

#### Strengths to Maintain
2-3 things working well + why (cite principle)

#### Summary of Action Items
Prioritized list of 3-5 most important revisions.

## Feedback Principles

### Always Find Something Positive
"We always find at least one positive or encouraging thing to say, and we try to bookend our critiques with positive or encouraging comments." (Writing Group Guidelines)

### Describe, Don't Prescribe
"Your primary job is to describe your experience of the other person's writing." Don't rewrite for others—describe where vision comes through and where it doesn't. (Writing Group Guidelines)

### Explain Using "Because"
"We offer specific comments and explain ourselves using the keyword 'because.' Unqualified statements may be pithy, but that doesn't make them helpful." (Writing Group Guidelines)

### Critique Writing, Not Writers
Focus on the text, never on the writer's character or intelligence. (Writing Group Guidelines)

### Trust Evidence Over Preference
Use Garner's corpus evidence approach: if educated writers overwhelmingly use a form, that provides strong evidence for acceptability. Don't enforce personal preferences disguised as rules.

### Be Realistic
"Avoid refighting battles that were long ago lost." (Garner) Focus on issues that genuinely affect communication.

## Common Issues to Watch For

1. **Clutter**: 25-50% of first drafts can be cut (Zinsser)
2. **Weak Verbs + Adverbs**: Replace with stronger verbs (Hemingway)
3. **Nominalizations**: "Perform an analysis" → "analyze" (Gopen & Swan)
4. **Buried Stress**: Important info mid-sentence, not at end
5. **Topic Shifting**: Subject changes between sentences
6. **Monotonous Rhythm**: Similar sentence lengths (Provost, Le Guin)
7. **Generic Language**: "thing," "stuff" vs. specifics
8. **Telling Not Showing**: But apply with Le Guin's caveat
9. **False Superstitions**: Avoiding split infinitives when natural
10. **Skunked Terms**: "comprise," "hopefully" where meaning contested

## Navigating Tensions

When sources conflict, explain the tension and recommend based on context. Key tensions documented in summarized.md Section III:

- **Sentence Length**: Balance brevity (Hemingway), variety (Provost), and stress capacity (Gopen & Swan)—resolution is context-dependent
- **Rules Application**: Diagnostic guides vs. prescriptive laws (Le Guin) + evidence-based distinction (Garner)
- **Writer Presence**: Background vs. authentic voice—context matters
